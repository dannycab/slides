# Grant Writing for STEM Education Research
## Turning Your Ideas into Funded Projects

# Why This Matters

## Research ideas are only part of doing research

- Our research can change how thousands of students learn
- But changing education at scale requires **resources**
- Grants fund equipment, personnel, summer salary, travel, partnerships
- Learning to write grants can amplify your impact

**Everyone can benefit from learning to write grants** -- they are persuasive writing

---

# The Grant Writing Paradox

**What people think grant writing is:**
- Writing beautiful prose to convince powerful people
- Luck + connections + privilege
- Something only "established" researchers do

**What it actually is:**
- **Matching your research to funder's priorities** (*this is a core skill*)
- Answering specific questions clearly
- Understanding what funders actually value
- Practice; it gets easier

# Part 1: Anatomy of a Proposal

## A grant proposal answers one question:

### "Why should we fund *your* work to solve *this* problem?"

**What reviewers actually care about:**
1. Is this a real problem?
2. Is your approach solid?
3. Will students/educators benefit?
4. Can you actually do this?

**Advice from Joe K: Always say WHY Michigan State is THE place for this work.**

---

# The Real Architecture of a Proposal

## Every proposal has three hidden layers:

### Layer 1: **Intellectual Merit**
*Why is this research important?*
- Is the research question significant?
- Is your approach novel or rigorous?
- Will it advance the field?

### Layer 2: **Broader Impacts**
*Who benefits beyond your lab?*
- How does this reach students, teachers, policy?
- Does it address equity or diversity?
- Is the societal value clear?

### Layer 3: **Fit with Funder Priorities**
*Does your work match what NSF wants to fund?*
- This determines if reviewers even care about layers 1 & 2

**Most common mistake: Forgetting layer 3.** Your beautiful research doesn't get funded if it doesn't match the program's goals.

# Reflection: What's Your Research Problem?

## Take 3 minutes. Write answers to these (on paper or in your head):

1. **What problem are you trying to solve?**
   - (Be specific: "How do students understand uncertainty?" not "Physics education is broken")

2. **Who is affected by this problem?**
   - (Teachers? Students? Both? Which students?)

3. **Why does it matter NOW?**
   - (Not "it's always been a problem" — what's urgent?)

4. **What would change if you solved it?**
   - (How many students? Which institutions? What's the scale?)

**Save these answers. We'll use them later.**

# Part 2: NSF Overview

## The National Science Foundation funds discovery AND education.

**NSF Budget: ~$9 billion/year**
- About 25% goes to STEM Education
- About 75% goes to disciplinary research

**For education research, key directorates:**
- **EDU** (Education & Human Resources) — where most of our awards live
- **DRL** (Division of Research on Learning) — cognition, learning sciences
- **CISE** (Computing) — computational thinking
- **ENG** (Engineering) — engineering education

**Key insight:** NSF funds innovation in education. They want to **transform teaching and learning at scale**, not just improve one classroom.

---

# NSF Review Criteria (The Magic Words)

## Every NSF proposal is judged on two criteria:

### Intellectual Merit
- Is the work technically sound?
- Does it advance knowledge in the field?
- Are the research questions important?

### Broader Impacts
- How does it benefit society?
- Does it reach diverse populations?
- Does it create lasting change?

**In STEM education:** Broader impacts = your primary contribution, not an afterthought.

---

# How NSF Actually Reviews Proposals

## Timeline (roughly):
1. **Proposal due** → NSF checks format
2. **NSF program officer reads it** → sends to reviewers
3. **3-4 peer reviewers** read and score (1-5 scale)
4. **NSF program officer synthesizes reviews** and makes funding decision
5. **Decision letter** — funded, declined, or request revision

## What reviewers look for:
- Is the problem clearly stated?
- Is this approach the right one?
- Will it actually work?
- Can the team do this?
- Does it match the program's priorities?

**You're not pitching to NSF. You're pitching to peers in your field who want to see your work succeed.**

# Deep Dive 1: IUSE (Improving Undergraduate STEM Education)

## One of the most common programs for education research.

**What it funds:**
- Curriculum development
- Faculty development
- Student learning research
- Assessment tools
- Institutional transformation

**Award sizes:**
- **Level 1** (Engaged Student Learning): $100K–$300K, 3 years
- **Level 2** (Institutional & Community Transformation): $300K–$800K, 4 years

**Who wins:**
- Single institutions + collaborators
- Projects with evidence of impact (faculty surveys, student learning data, etc.)
- Work that's **replicable and scalable** (can other institutions use it?)

---

# IUSE Success Pattern: From the Literature

## What makes a strong IUSE proposal?

**Evidence of a problem:**
- "Literature shows X% of students struggle with Y"
- "Faculty surveys indicate they need professional development in Z"
- "Current assessments don't measure A"

**A clear approach:**
- Summer workshops + sustained support (like PICUP)
- Assessment instrument development (like SPUCE)
- Curriculum materials + teacher training (like IC²S)

**Built-in sustainability:**
- Regional networks that outlast the grant
- Open-source materials others can adopt
- Faculty/teacher leadership from the start

**Diversity focus:**
- Does it reach MSIs, two-year colleges, underrepresented students?

---

# Example: One of Your IUSE Awards

## Award #2337055: PICUP Faculty Development Network

**The Problem:**
"Physics faculty want to teach computation, but lack resources and community support. Current integration is ad-hoc and rare."

**The Approach:**
- Regional workshops sustained by local leadership
- National community-building
- Assessment framework research
- Explicit focus on MSIs and two-year colleges

**Why it won (~$767K):**
- Addresses a **national need** with **local sustainability**
- Research informs practice (assessment framework)
- Reaches institutions usually excluded (TYCs, MSIs)
- Clear metrics (50% of physics departments, 25% of TYCs)

**Key insight:** This wasn't "let's run workshops." It was "here's how we scale a community to transform an entire field."

# Deep Dive 2: DRL (Division of Research on Learning)

## Different vibe. Focuses on learning science + practice.

**What it funds:**
- How do students actually learn?
- What teaching practices support learning?
- Learning in formal & informal settings
- Equity and access in learning

**Award sizes:**
- **Research on Learning**: $200K–$600K+, 3–5 years
- Often funds **research-practice partnerships** (university + schools)

**Who wins:**
- Teams that include teachers/practitioners, not just university researchers
- Projects with multiple research methods (surveys, video, assessments, interviews)
- Work that studies **equity explicitly**

---

# Example: One of Your DRL Awards

## Award #1741575: Integrating Computation in Science Across Michigan

**The Problem:**
"High school physics teaches classical mechanics, not 21st-century physics. Teachers lack support to teach computation equitably."

**The Approach:**
- Research-practice partnership: MSU + high school teachers
- Summer workshops + monthly support
- Three research strands:
  - Student Understanding (do they learn?)
  - Instructional Practice (how do teachers teach it?)
  - Equitable Participation (who's included? why/why not?)

**Why it won (~$1.36M):**
- **Teachers co-design** the work (not studied as objects)
- **Equity is central**, not added on
- **Multi-method research** (video + surveys + assessments)
- **Open-source materials** = broader impact

**Key insight:** DRL doesn't just want research. It wants research that **practitioners help create** and can actually use.

# Deep Dive 3: CAREER (Faculty Early-Career Development Program)

## The gold standard for early-career faculty.

**What it funds:**
- 5-year awards (~$600K–$1M)
- For faculty within 10 years of first R1 appointment
- **Integration of research + education** (both count, weighted differently by program)

**Award sizes:**
- Typically $600K–$1M over 5 years

**What reviewers look for:**
- **Bold vision** — not incremental
- **Integrated research + education plan** — they're connected
- **Potential for leadership** in field
- **Realistic timeline**

**Success pattern:**
- Research that opens a new direction + educational activities that flow from it
- Or research on learning that fundamentally changes how a field teaches

---

# Is CAREER Right for You?

## CAREER is NOT:
- For grad students (first appointment only)
- For established senior faculty
- Your first grant (usually)
- A guaranteed path to independence

## CAREER IS:
- Strategic — you're building a 5-year agenda
- Ambitious — reviewers expect bold vision
- Intentional — research + education are integrated
- Risky — some proposals are just too far ahead of their time

**If you're a grad student thinking ahead:** CAREER is the goal ~5–7 years from now, once you have pilot data and a research trajectory.

# Part 3: Translating Your Idea into a Fundable Proposal

## This is the hardest part. And the most important.

**The gap:**
- Your idea: "I want to understand how students learn X"
- Funder's question: "If we fund this, what changes?"

**Your job:** Build a bridge between your curiosity and their priorities.

---

# The Fundability Framework

## Ask yourself these questions (in order):

### 1. **What is the problem?**
   - Educational problem (not just research question)
   - Backed by evidence (lit review, pilot data, surveys)
   - Clear scope (not "education is broken")

### 2. **Why now?**
   - New tech enabling new approaches?
   - New policy creating urgency?
   - Gap that's becoming critical?
   - Field is ready for this work?

### 3. **Who will solve it?**
   - Do you have the expertise?
   - Do you need partners? (If yes, are they committed?)
   - Are there gaps you need to fill?

### 4. **What will you produce?**
   - Materials others can use?
   - Evidence that changes thinking?
   - New tools/assessments?
   - Trained educators?

### 5. **What funder cares about this?**
   - NSF program? Which one?
   - Private foundation? Which type?
   - Timeline match?

# Reflection: Your Fundable Problem

## Take 5 minutes. Reframe one of your research ideas.

**Start with:** Your current research question or idea

**Now answer:**
1. What **educational problem** does this solve? (Not: what will we discover? But: what will change for students/teachers?)
2. Why is NSF/a funder **likely to care** about this? (Hint: look at recent awards in your field)
3. Who else needs to be part of this? (Teachers? Other institutions? Industry?)
4. What will people **do with your results**? (Not: "advance the field" — be specific.)

**Share with someone next to you (or think aloud). What's the strongest answer?**

## Award #1913698: Student Understanding of Measurement Uncertainty

**Starting research question:**
"How do students understand measurement uncertainty in labs?"

**Fundable reframing:**
1. **Problem:** Most intro physics labs don't teach measurement uncertainty well; students leave without this essential skill.
2. **Why now:** No research-based assessment tool exists; curriculum is outdated.

# The Narrative Arc

## Every proposal tells a story. Shape it like this:

### **Act I: The Problem**
"Most high school physics teachers want to teach computation but don't know how. They report feeling unprepared [cite surveys]. Meanwhile, students graduate without 21st-century computational skills."

### **Act II: Your Solution**
"We will conduct a research-practice partnership with 36 Michigan teachers [specific]. Over two years, we'll develop professional development [summer + monthly], study its impact via three research lenses [student learning + instructional practice + equity], and create open-source resources."

### **Act III: The Payoff**
"By the end, teachers will have evidence-based practices for equitable computational instruction. Educators across the country will have free resources and a model to replicate."

**Key:** Each act connects to the next. It's not three separate things; it's one story with three parts.

---

# Intellectual Merit vs. Broader Impacts: The Balance

## NSF wants BOTH. But they're not the same.

### Intellectual Merit
- Your research question
- Your methods
- What the field learns

### Broader Impacts
- What educators do differently
- How students are reached
- Who's included
- Scale & sustainability

**In STEM education:**
- Intellectual merit: "We'll discover how teachers design computational instruction"
- Broader impacts: "This helps 36 teachers change practice for 1000 students; open resources reach teachers nationwide"

**Common mistake:** Treating them as separate sections. They should be **interwoven.** Your research question IS about understanding practice that transforms learning.

# The Nuts & Bolts: Project Description Structure

## NSF IUSE proposals typically have this shape:

1. **Introduction** (1–2 pages)
   - Problem statement
   - Why this matters
   - Brief roadmap of what you'll do

2. **Background & Significance** (2–3 pages)
   - Literature review
   - Gap you're filling
   - Why NSF should fund this (not someone else's problem)

3. **Research Questions & Approach** (4–6 pages)
   - What will you investigate?
   - How will you do it?
   - What are your methods?
   - Timeline

4. **Broader Impacts** (2–3 pages)
   - Who benefits?
   - How will you reach them?
   - Sustainability plan
   - Diversity/equity commitment

5. **Evaluation & Dissemination** (1–2 pages)
   - How will you know it worked?
   - How will you share results?

# Budget Reality Check

## NSF has rules. Know them.

**For IUSE Level 2 (~$300K–$800K over 4 years):**

Typical budget breakdown:
- **Personnel** (40–50%): Grad students, postdocs, summer salary for faculty
- **Workshops/Travel** (20–30%): Participant stipends, facility rental, travel for team
- **Materials & Supplies** (5–10%): Curriculum development, printing, tech
- **Indirect Costs** (~30%): Overhead (your institution takes this)

**Reality:** If your budget is $400K over 4 years, you have ~$280K in direct costs. That's:
- 0.5 FTE postdoc (~$100K salary+benefits = ~$50K/year = $200K total)
- 2 summer workshops for 30 people (~$10K each = $20K)
- 2 grad students at $500/month over 4 years = $48K
- Materials, travel, dissemination = $12K

**You're constrained.** Budget forces hard choices.

---

# Budget Narrative: How to Explain It

## Reviewers need to understand *why* you're spending *this much*.

**Bad:** "Salary support for one postdoc: $200,000"

**Good:** "We request 0.5 FTE postdoc ($50K/year = $200K total) to lead workshop facilitation, data collection, and analysis. This position is essential because [reason]. Without this support, [what fails?]"

**Example from your PICUP award:**
"We budget for 1.5 FTE staff to coordinate regional workshops across 6 regions and provide ongoing leadership development. Each region requires dedicated coordination to ensure sustainability; a single coordinator cannot manage this scope."

**Key:** Connect every budget line to **why it's necessary** to achieve your aims.

# The Project Summary (1 Page)

## This gets read by NSF staff AND reviewers. Make it sing.

**Structure:**
- **Sentence 1:** The problem (in plain English, non-jargon)
- **Sentences 2–3:** Why it matters (broader context)
- **Sentence 4:** Your approach (what will you DO?)
- **Sentence 5:** Expected outcomes (what will change?)
- **Sentence 6:** Broader impacts (who benefits, how?)

**Example (from your IC²S abstract):**
"High school physics courses rarely integrate computational modeling. This gap means students graduate without skills essential for 21st-century science and engineering. We propose a research-practice partnership with 36 Michigan teachers to develop professional development, study its impact, and create open-source resources. Teachers will gain competence in computational pedagogy; students will engage in authentic computational science. Results will be shared nationally, providing a replicable model for equitable integration of computation."

**Length:** ~250 words. Dense. No padding.

# When Your Idea Doesn't Fit NSF

## What if your work is too fundamental? Or too applied? Or too discipline-specific?

**NSF is not the only funder.**

### Private Foundations (30% of your strategy)
- Spencer Foundation (education research)
- MacArthur Foundation (broad interests)
- Sloan Foundation (STEM, especially computing)
- Mellon Foundation (higher learning)
- Motorola Foundation (diversity in tech)
- Regional community foundations

**Advantages:**
- Often faster decisions (4–6 months vs. 1–2 years for NSF)
- Different goals (innovation vs. rigor)
- Sometimes less competitive
- Can fund "small" things ($50K–$200K)

**Disadvantages:**
- Fewer $$$
- Narrower missions (might not fit)
- Less open-science expectations

# Private Funding: The Landscape

## Different types, different rhythms:

### Large National Foundations
- Spencer, MacArthur, Sloan, Mellon
- Long application processes
- Competitive
- $250K+

### Special-Purpose Foundations
- American Association of Physics Teachers (AAPT)
- American Educational Research Association (AERA)
- Subject-specific organizations
- Often rolling deadlines
- $10K–$50K

### Corporate/Corporate Foundations
- Motorola, Salesforce, Microsoft
- Often tied to company mission (diversity, rural access, tech)
- $25K–$500K

### University-Based Funding
- Your institution may have "internal grants" for pilot work
- Faculty development funds
- Good for trying small ideas before going to NSF

**Strategy:** Use private funding to **build pilot data**, then pitch NSF with evidence.

## How long does this actually take?

# Reviewing Your Own Proposal

## Before you hit submit, ask yourself:

- [ ] **Clarity:** Can a smart person in another field understand the problem?
- [ ] **Specificity:** Are your research questions *specific* (not "improve student learning" but "increase from 35% to 55% competency in X")?
- [ ] **Feasibility:** Can you actually do this in the time/budget?
- [ ] **Funder fit:** Did you mention the program explicitly? Why is this a good match?
- [ ] **Sustainability:** How does this change persist after funding ends?
- [ ] **Diversity:** Have you thought about who's included and who's not?
- [ ] **Dissemination:** How will others learn about this?

**If you answer "no" or "unclear" to any:** Go back. These are fixable.

# Common Mistakes (and How to Avoid Them)

## From reviewing proposals (and my own failures):

### **Mistake 1: No real problem**
*"We want to study how students learn by doing something new."*
**Fix:** Ground in evidence. "Surveys show X% of teachers don't use computational approaches; research shows students are disadvantaged by this gap."

### **Mistake 2: Idea without implementation**
*"We will create a framework for equitable teaching."*
**Fix:** Be concrete. "We will: (a) convene 15 teachers to identify barriers, (b) develop 5 classroom protocols, (c) train 20 teachers over two summers, (d) measure impact via student surveys + classroom video."

### **Mistake 3: Forgetting the audience**
*"This addresses an important gap in the literature."*
**Fix:** Say WHO benefits. "Teachers and students in rural schools, historically underrepresented in computational science, will..."

### **Mistake 4: Weak broader impacts**
*"Results will be disseminated through publications and conferences."*
**Fix:** Specific reach. "We will create open-source materials used by 500+ educators; develop a training pipeline reaching 50 new faculty yearly."

### **Mistake 5: Underselling the research**
*"We'll use surveys and interviews."*
**Fix:** Say WHY those methods. "We use mixed methods (surveys for scale, interviews for depth, video for practice) to triangulate findings."

# The Proposal Review Process: What Happens After You Submit

## Weeks 1–4: Administratively complete?
- NSF checks format, budget, conflicts of interest
- If incomplete, you get a chance to fix it

## Weeks 5–12: Out to reviewers
- Program officer sends your proposal to 3–4 peers
- They have 4 weeks to review
- They score you 1 (poor) — 5 (excellent) on both criteria

## Week 13–16: Program officer decision
- Reads all reviews
- Makes recommendation to NSF leadership
- Decision: Fund, Decline, or Request Revisions

## Week 17+: You get the letter
- If funded: Congratulations! Start in ~1–2 months.
- If declined: Reviews are attached (painful but useful)
- If revise/resubmit: You have ~1 month to address concerns

**Total:** ~4–5 months from submission to decision.

# If You're Declined (You Will Be. All of Us Are.)

## The first review hurts. The second and third get easier.

**What to do:**
1. **Read the reviews carefully.** (Painful step.)
2. **Don't defend yourself.** The reviewers aren't wrong; they're giving feedback.
3. **Identify the top 2–3 concerns.** (Not all 15.)
4. **Plan next steps:**
   - Can you fix these for a revision?
   - Should you pitch a different funder?
   - Is your idea not ready yet? (Pilot more? Get more preliminary data?)

**About resubmission:**
- NSF allows resubmission same program next cycle
- Your new version should cite and address the prior reviews
- If you resubmit, reviewers often include at least one reviewer who saw v1
- Address their concerns directly

**Advice:** Declined is not defeat. It's information.

# Working with a Program Officer

## These people are your allies. Use them.

**What program officers do:**
- Manage competitions
- Help you find the right program
- Clarify what's fundable vs. not
- Sometimes advocate for your proposal

**You can call them (really!):**

*"Hi Dr. Chen, I'm Danny Caballero from Michigan State. I have an idea for high school teacher professional development in computational physics. Is IUSE a good fit, or should I look at STEM+C? Do you have 15 minutes to talk?"*

**They will say yes.** That's literally their job.

**What to ask:**
- "Is my idea in scope for this program?"
- "What are you looking for this cycle?"
- "I'm planning to pitch X. What would make this stronger?"

**What NOT to do:**
- Share your full proposal before submission
- Ask them to review (that's what peer review is for)
- Bother them weekly

# Key Takeaways

## You don't need to remember all the details. Remember these:

1. **Grants match ideas to funders.** Your job is to translate YOUR curiosity into THEIR priorities.

2. **NSF funds scale and evidence.** They want work that transforms how educators teach and students learn, not just one classroom.

3. **The architecture is learnable.** Problem → Approach → Impact → Funder fit. Once you see it, you can replicate it.

4. **Your first proposal won't be your best.** But it'll get better. Everyone here has declines in their past.

5. **You have time.** If you're a grad student, this is practice. If you're a postdoc, this is your next 5 years. If you're a faculty member, this is ongoing.

6. **You're not alone.** Your colleagues have done this. Your program officer wants you to win. Peer reviewers (usually) want good work to succeed.
