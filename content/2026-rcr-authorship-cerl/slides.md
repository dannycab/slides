# Authorship, Paper Development & Research Readiness  
### CERL group meeting (30 January 2026)

**Goals today**
- Understand formal authorship criteria (ICMJE / *Science*, Nature, PLOS ONE). 
- Practice authorship decisions & ordering using realistic scenarios.
- Frame how ideas become publishable papers & judge readiness.

---

## Why Authorship Matters

- Credit, responsibility, and accountability are tied to authorship.
- Expectations differ by journal & field; transparency is increasingly required.
- Our work often involves software, tools, and curriculum artifacts & models vary in how they credit these.

## Formal Models We Can Use

- **ICMJE**: widely used; aligns with many policies used by *Science*, strict 4-criteria model.<sup>[1](https://www.icmje.org/recommendations/browse/roles-and-responsibilities/defining-the-role-of-authors-and-contributors.html)</sup>
- **Nature Portfolio**: strong emphasis on explicit contribution statements & agreement on order.<sup>[2](https://www.nature.com/documents/nature-manuscript-checklist-research.pdf)</sup>
- **PLOS ONE**: more inclusive of software & writing‑only substantial contributions.<sup>[3](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/s/authorship)</sup> Based on McNutt et al. (2018)<sup>[4](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1715374115)</sup>

## ICMJE: The Four Criteria<sup>[1](https://www.icmje.org/recommendations/browse/roles-and-responsibilities/defining-the-role-of-authors-and-contributors.html)</sup>

An author must meet **all**:
1) Substantial contribution to conception/design **or** data acquisition/analysis/interpretation  
2) Drafting the work **or** critical revision  
3) Final approval of the version to be published  
4) Agreement to be accountable for all aspects of the work

> Implication: “only coded” or “only collected data” ≠ authorship unless other criteria are also met. 

---

## Nature Portfolio: Transparency & Agreement<sup>[2](https://www.nature.com/documents/nature-manuscript-checklist-research.pdf)</sup>

- Requires an **Author Contribution Statement** specifying each author’s role.
- All authors should agree on **order** and on **submission** before submission.
- Non‑author contributors should be acknowledged.

> Focus: *documented transparency* more than a rigid, universal threshold.

---

## PLOS ONE: Inclusive & Role‑Sensitive<sup>[3](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/s/authorship)</sup>

Authors must have:
- **Substantial contributions** to conception/design; data acquisition/analysis; **software creation**; **or** substantial writing/revision, **and**
- **Approved** the submitted version, **and**
- **Agreed** to be accountable for their own contributions

> Useful where software tools and major writing can justify authorship.

---

## Quick Compare

- **ICMJE**<sup>[1](https://www.icmje.org/recommendations/browse/roles-and-responsibilities/defining-the-role-of-authors-and-contributors.html)</sup>: Strict; all 4 criteria; guards against “guest authorship.” 
- **Nature**<sup>[2](https://www.nature.com/documents/nature-manuscript-checklist-research.pdf)</sup>: Demands explicit contribution statements & consensus on order. 
- **PLOS ONE**<sup>[3](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/s/authorship)</sup>: Inclusive of software & substantial writing alone; aligns with multi‑modal research and products.

**Which best fits your current project?**

## CRediT Roles (14 Contributor Roles)<sup>[5](https://credit.niso.org/)</sup>

- Conceptualization, Data curation, Formal analysis,
- Funding acquisition, Investigation, Methodology, Project administration,
- Resources, Software, Supervision, Validation, Visualization,
- Writing – original draft, Writing – review & editing

> Clarifies contributions; complements authorship decisions.

## Is the Paper Ready?

**Indicators of readiness**
- Clear research question & aligned contribution
- Methods replicable and/or validated; analysis complete & interpretable
- Claims supported; limitations acknowledged
- All authors have meaningfully contributed, approved, and accept accountability (per venue)

## Why Ordering Matters (STEM Ed Context)

- Signals magnitude/leadership of contribution  
- First ≈ led the work; Last ≈ senior oversight (varies by field)  
- Hybrid norms in our field (influences from CS, education, social sciences)  
> Early, explicit discussion prevents conflict; document expectations.

## Good Practice: When to Decide & How to Record

- Discuss authorship **early**; revisit at milestones (post‑data collection, post‑analysis)  
- Keep a living contributions log mapped to **CRediT**  
- Ensure everyone reviews the **final manuscript** and agrees on submission & order (Nature/PLOS)

---

## Authorship Order Can Vary by Discipline & Venue

* **Astronomy & Astrophysics (small)**: Ordered by contribution; first author leads and handles submission; last author has no meaning.
* **Astronomy & Astrophysics (large)**: First few by contribution, rest alphabetical; first author handles submission; last author has no meaning.
* **High Energy Physics**: Fully alphabetical author list; designated corresponding author manages review and submission.
* **Most Physics Subfields**: First author is lead; last author is senior mentor/PI and carries status.
* **Applied Math & Computer Science**: Small papers alphabetical; larger/applied papers often by contribution, with first author submitting.
---

## Takeaways

- Authorship = **contribution + responsibility** (ICMJE baseline).
- **Transparency** via contribution statements/CRediT is now standard (Nature/PLOS)
- Decide early, document often, and align with the **target venue’s** policy.
